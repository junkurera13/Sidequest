# Sidequest Vision

**Status:** Living product vision  
**Last updated:** July 21, 2026

## The idea in one line

> **Sidequest finds experiences that feel strangely meant for you.**

Sidequest is an agent that composes meaningful real-world experiences for people. It understands who someone is, who matters to them, what has made them feel alive before, and what their present circumstances allow. It uses that understanding to create experiences that are personal, surprising, practical, and worth remembering.

Sidequest is not a marketplace, recommendation feed, itinerary generator, or chatbot that produces lists of nearby businesses. The ambition is to create something artistic, seamless, simple, and deeply human: a product in which AI becomes invisible and the experience it creates becomes the artwork.

The larger inspiration is an agentic product that could have emerged from Airbnb's original belief in meaningful travel and human connection, taken beyond homes and bookable experiences. Sidequest is not concerned only with where someone stays or what inventory is available. It is concerned with helping someone live more fully.

## Why Sidequest should exist

People often have time without truly using it.

Busy people, in particular, tend to remain busy even when a free window appears. Staying home has no activation cost. Going somewhere requires choosing an activity, researching it, coordinating people, checking logistics, getting ready, and accepting the possibility that it may not be enjoyable. The couch often wins before a conscious decision is made.

Yet once someone crosses that threshold, they may have a beautiful time. The real obstacle was not a lack of interest. It was uncertainty, inertia, and the effort required to begin.

Sidequest should remove that activation energy. It should sometimes be able to say:

> saturday afternoon is open. i made something for you and your family. leave at 3:40.

The plan is already waiting. The person does not need to browse, compare, coordinate, or become the planner.

Two complementary descriptions of the product are:

> **Sidequest notices when life has space and places something meaningful inside it.**

> **Sidequest turns unclaimed time into something worth remembering.**

## The origin insight

The central insight came from a trip to Fukuoka with close friends.

One of its strongest memories was cycling for roughly ninety minutes around an unfamiliar island town. Cycling was already loved and familiar. The location, environment, and unplanned discovery were unfamiliar. Close friends made the exploration meaningful and emotionally safe. Photographs preserved the experience and still produce nostalgia.

The magic did not come from any single ingredient. It came from their composition.

This suggests that memorable human experiences often contain a balance between what is already part of someone and what sits just beyond their current world. The familiar gives confidence, identity, or safety. The unfamiliar creates discovery and expansion.

This is Sidequest's private creative doctrine.

### The magician rule

The balance between the known and unknown must remain backstage. Sidequest should never explain the mechanism blatantly to the user. Doing so would be like a magician explaining the trick while performing it.

The person should experience the result, not the framework that produced it.

Sidequest may reflect the emotional details of a memory:

> cycling through some town u never planned to see with your favorite people. yeah, that one stays.

It should not respond with an analytical explanation such as:

> Cycling was familiar, the island was unfamiliar, and your friends created emotional safety.

Similarly, Sidequest should not explain a recommendation with preference scores or causal logic. The choice should initially feel surprising and then make sense in retrospect.

We hide the creative machinery, not practical information. Price, distance, opening hours, accessibility, safety, permissions, and genuine uncertainty must remain transparent.

## The two pillars

### 1. People

Great experiences are shaped by the people inside them:

- Friends, including the different energy of childhood, school, university, work, or newer friendships.
- Family.
- Partners.
- Solitude.
- Strangers who may eventually become friends or lovers.

The category alone is insufficient. Sidequest should gradually understand what a person's presence enables:

- Friends who make someone braver or more spontaneous.
- Family members who create comfort, belonging, or nostalgia.
- A partner who makes an ordinary setting feel intimate.
- A group with whom the person can be silly and unguarded.
- People the user feels responsible for entertaining.
- Strangers who make the world feel unexpectedly welcoming.

People and experiences are not independent variables. Trusted people can make an unfamiliar activity feel possible. An unfamiliar person may feel more approachable in a familiar setting. The composition of social safety and discovery matters.

The initial product should focus primarily on experiences alone or with people the user already knows. Meeting strangers, friendship, romance, and group matching are meaningful future branches, but they introduce substantial questions of trust, consent, safety, and moderation.

### 2. Experiences

A Sidequest is not simply a venue. It is an authored real-world experience with an emotional shape.

Sidequest should understand and compose from elements such as:

- The activity and setting.
- Place, time, weather, duration, travel, and cost.
- Familiar interests and capabilities.
- Meaningful discovery or novelty.
- Pace, physicality, energy, spontaneity, and agency.
- Sensory qualities and atmosphere.
- The people involved.
- Emotional safety and personal constraints.
- The moment of surprise, intimacy, freedom, achievement, or belonging.
- The memory that may remain afterwards.

A great Sidequest may contain several places, but it may also be one bike, one ferry, and one island. The current product's rule requiring exactly three stops is an implementation artifact and should be removed.

## AI as art

Sidequest should demonstrate that AI can create art without the artwork being an AI-generated picture.

The artwork is the experience it composes: the choice, sequence, timing, people, atmosphere, and emotional possibility. The agent's reasoning should feel like taste rather than calculation.

The user should not receive twenty ranked options. Sidequest should make one confident proposal, with graceful ways to accept, adjust, move, or decline it.

The product succeeds when someone thinks:

> How did it know this was exactly right for me?

The more Sidequest understands someone, the quieter the product should become.

## The personal experience graph

After hearing a meaningful memory, Sidequest creates the beginning of a private, evolving model of the person.

Internally, this can be called the **personal experience graph**. A future user-facing expression might be called **your world** or **your living atlas**, though the internal analysis should remain hidden.

This is not a conventional profile made of permanent labels such as `introvert`, `foodie`, or `likes cycling`. It is a living web of people, places, activities, emotions, memories, contexts, and relationships between them.

The graph should preserve three layers:

### The original memory

The exact source material is retained: the person's raw text, voice note, photograph, or other artifact. Sidequest should never discard the human story after converting it into structured data.

### What Sidequest knows

Facts that the person stated directly or that are strongly supported by the memory. For example:

- The user already loved cycling.
- The experience happened with close friends.
- The location was unfamiliar.
- The photographs continue to produce nostalgia.

### What Sidequest suspects

Creative hypotheses that may help compose future experiences but must not harden into stereotypes. For example:

- The user may enjoy active exploration with trusted people.
- Spontaneity may contribute to their enjoyment.
- Visual memories may be especially meaningful to them.
- Unstructured discovery may matter more than famous destinations.

Hypotheses should retain confidence, supporting evidence, and the ability to weaken or disappear. One memory should never permanently declare that someone loves islands, dislikes tours, or always wants company.

Nationality, demographics, and other broad attributes may provide faint context, but they must not replace learned personal knowledge. "They told us they love small breweries" is meaningful evidence. "They are German, therefore they like beer" is only a weak stereotype and must not drive the experience.

## First-use onboarding

Onboarding should feel like the beginning of a relationship, not the completion of a form.

There should be no interest checklist, personality quiz, visible profile percentage, or series of demographic questions. The person gives Sidequest one meaningful memory as a natural dump.

An initial invitation could be:

> tell me about a real day you'd live again — the people, what happened, and why it stayed. messy is good.

The user should be able to respond through text, a voice note, a photograph, or a combination. They may write as much or as little as they want.

From that single response, Sidequest attempts to understand:

- Who was present and what those relationships meant.
- What actually happened.
- The setting and atmosphere.
- What was already familiar.
- What was discovered.
- Why the combination worked.
- The emotions created during and after the experience.
- Any boundaries, friction, or constraints the person mentions.
- The deeper pattern that could be preserved without repeating the same surface activity.

Sidequest should not expect to understand the entire person from one memory. This is the first root, not a completed profile. Unknowns remain unknown and are learned naturally over time.

### Immediate conversation, background understanding

The conversation should never pause while the full personal experience graph is constructed.

The system has two layers:

- **Frontstage:** a fast, natural conversation that remains responsive.
- **Backstage:** slower interpretation, graph construction, confidence assessment, checking, and persistence.

After the memory arrives:

1. Sidequest preserves the raw memory immediately.
2. It begins deep interpretation in the background.
3. A fast pass creates a specific emotional acknowledgement.
4. Sidequest immediately asks for the first available window.
5. The user continues responding while the experience graph is constructed.
6. The graph only needs to be ready before the actual experience is composed.

Example:

> **Sidequest:** cycling through some town u never planned to see with your favorite people. yeah, that one stays.

Then, after a natural beat:

> **Sidequest:** when do u have a few hours free? i want to make your first sidequest.

The user can answer naturally:

- `saturday afternoon`
- `tomorrow after work`
- `sometime sunday with my wife`
- `i'm off next week`

Sidequest interprets the window and asks only for context that is truly missing and capable of materially changing the experience:

> where will u be?

Or:

> who's coming, or is this one just u?

Once Sidequest has a usable time window, a location, the participants, and any explicitly stated hard constraint, the user is ready for their first Sidequest.

If background interpretation fails, the original memory remains available. The system should retry silently and fall back gracefully without exposing a technical error to the person.

## The first Sidequest

The first Sidequest is a handshake. It is Sidequest's first opportunity to prove that it understood something real.

It should not reproduce the person's favorite memory literally. Someone who talks about cycling in Fukuoka should not automatically be sent cycling again. Sidequest should preserve a deeper quality while changing the surface.

The first experience should generally be:

- One to a few hours long.
- Affordable or at least clear about cost.
- Easy to leave if it is not working.
- Logistically straightforward.
- Possible alone or with existing friends or family.
- Specific enough that it could not have come from a generic city guide.
- Verified as a real and currently viable experience.
- Distinctive enough to establish trust in Sidequest's taste.

Behind the scenes, the agent should:

1. Read the personal experience graph and original evidence.
2. Understand the immediate window and participants.
3. Research current real-world possibilities.
4. Compose one experience with an intentional emotional arc.
5. Critique its own proposal.
6. Reject anything generic, impractical, unsafe, or insufficiently personal.
7. Verify logistics and factual details.
8. Send one confident invitation.

The invitation should create anticipation without explaining the hidden thesis:

> found something for saturday
>
> wear shoes u don't mind getting dirty

## iMessage and the larger product

iMessage is a strong starting surface, but it should not contain the entire product.

It is valuable because it provides:

- No-install conversational access.
- The intimacy of an ongoing contact rather than a software dashboard.
- Natural questions, answers, photographs, voice notes, and invitations.
- A place for quiet, proactive messages once trust has been earned.
- Future potential for coordination with friends and groups.

However, the full Sidequest vision needs a surface the product owns. Rich reveals, progress, maps, photographs, memories, fluid motion, and the living atlas cannot be expressed completely inside a text thread.

The intended model is:

- **iMessage:** the conversation, invitation, and quiet companion.
- **Mobile Sidequest experience:** the reveal and active real-world experience.
- **Personal atlas:** the person's memories and expanding world.

The experience is not the text conversation. The conversation opens the door.

The underlying intelligence and memory should remain independent of iMessage so that future surfaces can include a native app, web, WhatsApp, or other channels without fragmenting the person's history.

## The reveal and active experience

The mobile reveal should not be a generic stack of itinerary cards. It should feel authored for this person and this moment.

It may include:

- An evocative title.
- A restrained, personal introduction.
- When to leave.
- Where to begin.
- Duration and realistic cost.
- What to bring.
- Essential reservation, weather, accessibility, or safety information.
- One clear action: **begin**.

Not every part of the experience must be exposed immediately. Sidequest may unfold moments as they happen, preserving discovery while remaining transparent about any logistics that require preparation or consent.

During the experience, Sidequest should be quiet. It may provide a necessary direction, adapt when plans fail, or answer a natural message such as:

- `this place is packed`
- `i'm tired`
- `can we skip the food part?`
- `one more drink somewhere quiet?`

It should disappear when life is happening.

## Memory and learning

The old `W/L` outcome is too reductive for this vision. A human experience is not simply won or lost.

Sidequest should close an experience with a small, meaningful invitation such as:

> what part are u still thinking about?

Or:

> send me the photo that feels like today

The response becomes evidence of what actually mattered. This is more valuable than clicks or completion status.

The core learning loop is:

> memory → understanding → invitation → experience → reflection → deeper understanding

Over time, the unknown becomes part of the person's world. New interests, relationships, settings, and possibilities create further branches. The graph grows like roots or a constellation, but the person sees their life rather than the system's analysis.

## Proactivity and free time

Sidequest should eventually be capable of noticing available time and preparing an experience before the person asks.

This must be permission-based. An empty calendar does not always mean someone is emotionally available, and rest must remain valid. Sidequest should never turn leisure into another productivity system or make someone feel guilty for staying home.

The first version can ask directly:

> when do u have a few hours free? i want to make your first sidequest.

Later, a person may grant recurring **Sidequest windows**:

> sundays after 3 are usually ours. if u find something good, make the plan.

Much later, an optional Google Calendar connection could identify holidays, unusually open days, or shared availability. Only calendar access is needed for this; Sidequest should not request Gmail inbox access merely to understand time. The product should read the minimum information necessary and explain permissions clearly.

Proactivity must be earned. Before trust, a message feels like a notification. After trust, it can feel like someone thought of you.

## Agency, responsibility, and rest

Sidequest should create momentum without coercion.

The experience can arrive prepared and specific, but the person must always have graceful options to:

- Accept.
- Move it.
- Adjust a meaningful constraint.
- Decline without guilt.

Sometimes the right answer is rest. Sidequest should learn the difference between genuine recovery and inertia without claiming perfect knowledge of the person.

High-stakes uncertainty must not be romanticized. The product must remain responsible about safety, accessibility, health, travel, strangers, alcohol, cost, and the reliability of real-world information.

## Product character

Sidequest should feel like an unusually perceptive person in the user's contacts, not a chatbot performing a personality.

The voice should be:

- Concise.
- Warm without becoming sentimental.
- Specific rather than generic.
- Confident without pretending certainty.
- Casual without forced slang.
- Curious without interrogating.
- Quiet when nothing needs to be said.

Lowercase text or any specific verbal style is not sacred. The final voice must be discovered and tested as part of the larger product character.

The visual identity can be renovated completely. The current pixel-game styling, mission-file language, stacked dark cards, and nostalgic interface are not constraints. The future design may become organic, cinematic, spatial, and alive, but it should emerge from the emotional identity of Sidequest rather than from a fashionable visual treatment.

## Design direction

Design is part of the product's meaning, not a decorative layer added after the agent works.

The reference point is an agentic product with Airbnb's warmth, hospitality, editorial clarity, generous spacing, human photography, and calm confidence, combined with Apple's responsiveness, spatial consistency, physical interaction, and restrained motion.

The working rule is:

> **Airbnb is the reference for emotional temperature and composition. Apple is the reference for behavior and motion. Sidequest must remain its own identity.**

This means learning from the qualities of those products without cloning protected brand elements or producing an imitation that could be mistaken for either company. Sidequest should use properly licensed typography, its own signature color, its own iconography, and an original visual language.

### Visual thesis

Sidequest should feel warm, human, editorial, spacious, and quietly cinematic. Real places, people, light, texture, and memory should provide the visual richness. Interface chrome should remain restrained.

The system should favor:

- Warm off-whites and natural neutral surfaces instead of harsh pure white or permanent black.
- Deep ink text with excellent contrast.
- One distinctive Sidequest accent rather than multiple competing brand colors.
- Large, emotionally resonant photography that does narrative work.
- Generous whitespace and disciplined alignment.
- Soft geometry where it supports approachability, without turning every section into a rounded card.
- No gradients, glass, shadows, or decorative effects unless they communicate atmosphere, hierarchy, or physical depth.
- At most two typefaces, with optical sizing, tracking, and leading tuned for each scale.

The old neon yellow, pink, green, scanlines, pixel shadows, and pixel type are not part of the new direction.

### Interaction thesis

Motion should make Sidequest feel attentive and alive without calling attention to animation itself.

- Feedback begins immediately on touch or press.
- Interactive objects track gestures directly and remain interruptible.
- Calm, critically damped springs are the default; bounce is reserved for genuine momentum.
- Reveals originate from the object or invitation that caused them.
- Enter and exit paths remain spatially consistent.
- Large transitions are sparse and meaningful.
- Reduced-motion, reduced-transparency, and increased-contrast preferences are first-class behavior.
- The interface should become quiet once the real-world experience begins.

The reveal of a Sidequest should be designed as one composition, not assembled from a dashboard grid. The first viewport should have one dominant image or atmospheric visual, one clear promise, and one primary action.

### Design standard

Every visible choice should be defensible: type, spacing, crop, transition, timing, hierarchy, language, and touch behavior. Delight should emerge from purpose, clarity, and craft rather than ornamental effects.

The intended result is not "an app that looks like Airbnb" or "an Apple-style website." It is a Sidequest experience with comparable care, calm, and coherence.

## What Sidequest is not

Sidequest is not:

- A marketplace of bookable inventory.
- A list of nearby restaurants and attractions.
- A city guide with AI-generated copy.
- A feed designed to maximize screen time.
- A rigid three-stop itinerary generator.
- A gamified streak or achievement system.
- A productivity system for leisure.
- A chatbot that asks unnecessary questions.
- A personality quiz disguised as onboarding.
- A system that stereotypes users from demographics.
- A product that exposes its psychological analysis to create the appearance of intelligence.
- A product whose visual polish attempts to compensate for mediocre experiences.

## Product principles

1. **The experience is the artwork.**
2. **Make one thoughtful choice instead of presenting a catalogue.**
3. **Hide the creative machinery; disclose practical truth and risk.**
4. **Learn through stories and lived outcomes, not only declared preferences.**
5. **Preserve the original human memory alongside structured understanding.**
6. **Treat hypotheses as hypotheses, never permanent identity labels.**
7. **Use people as part of the emotional composition, not merely attendee counts.**
8. **Remove activation energy without removing agency.**
9. **Rest is valid. Declining is not failure.**
10. **The more Sidequest understands, the quieter it becomes.**
11. **Never send someone somewhere generic merely because it is nearby.**
12. **Build block by block around a complete human experience.**

## Decisions already made

- A complete renovation of the existing product is acceptable.
- The old visual identity and interaction model do not need to be preserved.
- Monetization and marketplace mechanics are not current priorities.
- The foundational pillars are people and experiences.
- The known–unknown principle remains private and invisible.
- Onboarding begins with one open-ended memory dump rather than a questionnaire.
- The initial experience graph is created silently in the background.
- The conversation continues immediately while that deeper processing happens.
- The next onboarding question asks when the person is free for their first Sidequest.
- The first version should be developed block by block.
- iMessage is the initial conversational doorway, not the entire long-term product.
- The fixed three-stop structure and W/L outcome model should not survive the renovation.

## Open product questions

- Should the first tested experiences focus on everyday life in the user's own city, travel, or support both from the beginning?
- What city and geographic boundary should the earliest high-quality testing use?
- What exact voice should Sidequest have?
- What does the first mobile reveal look and feel like?
- How much of an experience is revealed in advance versus during it?
- How should location be provided and consented to in the initial iMessage flow?
- What minimum practical context is required before composition begins?
- How much human review, if any, should exist during the earliest beta?
- What is the right balance between decisiveness and easy adjustment?
- How should the personal atlas eventually appear without exposing the analytical machinery?

## Small roadmap

The roadmap is intentionally organized into complete product blocks rather than a long feature list.

### Block 1 — Memory onboarding

Create the first relationship-defining interaction:

- Replace the old onboarding flow.
- Invite one open-ended memory through text, voice, or photo.
- Preserve the original memory.
- Generate a fast, story-specific emotional acknowledgement.
- Build the deeper personal experience graph asynchronously.
- Ask for the user's first free window immediately.
- Capture only missing location, participant, or hard-constraint context.
- Make the flow resilient when analysis is slow or fails.

**Proof:** A new user can give one natural dump and feel accurately heard without completing a form or waiting for analysis.

### Block 2 — First experience composer

Build the first act of taste:

- Define the initial geographic and temporal scope.
- Research specific, current, real-world possibilities.
- Compose one experience rather than a list.
- Add an internal critic that rejects generic or impractical output.
- Verify location, hours, cost, travel, weather, and relevant constraints.
- Remove the fixed three-stop requirement.
- Preserve facts and uncertainty separately from creative language.

**Proof:** Sidequest consistently produces an experience that feels specific to the person and viable in the real world.

### Block 3 — Mobile reveal

Create the first beautiful owned surface:

- Replace the old mission card.
- Design an authored, mobile-first reveal.
- Present preparation and logistics clearly.
- Preserve mystery where appropriate.
- Provide one clear beginning action.
- Support adaptation when the real world changes.

**Proof:** Opening a Sidequest feels like receiving something made for you, not reading an itinerary.

### Block 4 — Reflection and evolving understanding

Close the loop:

- Replace W/L with a photograph, voice note, or reflective response.
- Connect outcomes to the original experience and participants.
- Update facts and hypotheses with evidence.
- Allow weak assumptions to fade.
- Begin exploring a private, beautiful memory atlas.

**Proof:** Sidequest becomes observably better after a lived experience without asking the user to manage a profile.

### Block 5 — Permission-based proactivity

Only after the core loop earns trust:

- Add one-time and recurring Sidequest windows.
- Learn when invitations are welcome.
- Support moving or declining without pressure.
- Explore minimal Google Calendar availability access.
- Compose occasional experiences before the user asks.

**Proof:** A proactive Sidequest feels like someone thought of the person, not like another notification.

### Later horizons

- Native iPhone experience with deeper motion, haptics, location, photography, and notifications.
- Shared calendars and coordination among friends or family.
- Group Sidequests.
- Travel-aware experiences and longer journeys.
- Carefully designed encounters with strangers.
- Friendship and romantic possibility, with serious safety and consent systems.
- Reservations or purchases where they materially reduce friction.
- A mature living atlas showing how someone's world has expanded.

## The standard

Sidequest should aspire to the coherence, restraint, humanity, and craft associated with the best work of Jony Ive and Steve Jobs—not by copying Apple's visual language, but by making the technology disappear behind an experience with a singular purpose.

Beautiful typography and motion will matter. So will language, timing, reliability, privacy, and every small transition. But visual craft cannot rescue an experience that is generic, factually unreliable, unsafe, or emotionally empty.

The standard is not that Sidequest looks futuristic.

The standard is that someone goes somewhere they would not have gone, with someone who matters, experiences a moment they could not have planned for themselves, and later looks at a photograph wishing they could step back inside it.
